Dense, Sparse, and Hybrid Retrieval

Retrieval systems find the documents most relevant to a query. There are two broad
families of methods: dense and sparse.

Dense retrieval represents both the query and the documents as embedding vectors in
a continuous space and measures similarity with a metric such as cosine similarity.
Because embeddings capture meaning, dense retrieval can match a query to a passage
even when they share no exact words, which makes it strong at semantic matching.

Sparse retrieval represents text as high-dimensional sparse vectors over the
vocabulary and scores documents with term-based methods such as BM25. BM25 weights
matches by term frequency and inverse document frequency. Sparse retrieval excels at
exact keyword matching, rare terms, and precise names or identifiers.

Hybrid retrieval combines dense and sparse scores to get the best of both: the
semantic recall of embeddings and the lexical precision of BM25. The two score lists
are usually merged with a fusion method such as reciprocal rank fusion.

After retrieval, a reranking step can further improve precision. A reranker is a
cross-encoder that jointly reads the query and each candidate passage and assigns a
relevance score, reordering the candidates so the most relevant ones come first.
Reranking is more accurate than the first-stage retriever but too expensive to run
over the whole corpus, so it is applied only to the top candidates.
